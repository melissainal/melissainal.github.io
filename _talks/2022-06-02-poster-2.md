Abstract:

Drosophila neuromuscular junction (NMJ) has been used for decades to study neurodevelopment and function. Its amenability to genetic tricks and evolutionary conservation of molecular and cellular mechanisms renders the NMJ as a preferred model system for studying synaptogenesis. The precision of partner recognition is crucial to the formation of synapses in a specific manner for normal development of the NMJ. For instance, in the embryonic stage, filopodia at the tips of growth cones, which are actin-based membrane protrusions, lead NMJ formation by finding their appropriate synaptic partners. While conventional fluorescence microscopy allows enough resolution to recognize some of the filopodia before the intricate filopodial contact is made, the diffraction limit of light prevents further analysis in later phases, as filopodia tend to be 100-200 nm in width. On the other hand, single molecule localization microscopy (SMLM) can take advantage of photo-switchable properties of fluorescent dyes to localize each fluorophore with high precision. In this study, we optimize 2-color SMLM imaging and study the synaptic development at the NMJ in vivo. Using our optimized approach, we observe the dynamic changes in the filopodia structure and widths during development, which can be seen due to filopodial contact. We also observe specific localization of synaptic proteins, suggesting the localization of these proteins may act as a scaffold for filopodial protrusion. Altogether, these data provide us an opportunity to closely study the molecular mechanisms involved in synaptic formation and ask questions about the functions of cytoskeleton, which provide the filopodial structure in their dynamic roles, using 2-color super-resolution microscopy.
